Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Equity Securities - Common Stock
Common stock represents the most basic type of stock, which represents ownership in a company. It is also known as ordinary shares. Common stockholders are entitled to one vote for each share owned on matters to be decided at stockholders' meetings and receive dividends as may be declared by the board of directors out of funds legally available therefore. In the event of liquidation, dissolution or winding up of a company, common stockholders are entitled to receive ratably such amounts as may be available for distribution after payment of liabilities and after each class of stock, if any, having preference over the common stock.
Common stock is considered the most basic type of stock because it represents the residual ownership in a company after deducting all other claims on its assets. The claims of common stockholders are subordinate to those of creditors, including banks, as well as any preferred stockholders. Common stockholders generally control the management of the company by electing its board of directors and by voting on corporate policy.
Common stock is considered to be a high-risk investment and is generally unsuitable for risk-averse investors. The value of common stock may be subject to significant fluctuations in response to numerous factors, many of which are beyond the control of the company, including economic conditions generally and industry conditions specifically.
Common stock is quoted on stock exchanges and can be bought and sold through brokers. The price of common stock is determined by supply and demand in the stock market. The value of common stock may be affected by the operating performance of the company as well as other economic, business and political factors.
Common stock is often issued by companies in initial public offerings (IPOs) to raise capital for financing their operations. It is also issued by companies in connection with mergers and acquisitions and employee stock option plans.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Equity Securities - Preferred Stock
Preferred stock is a type of stock that has a higher claim on assets and earnings than common stock. It is often referred to as "preferred" because it has preference over common stock in the distribution of assets in the event of liquidation. Preferred stockholders also generally have rights senior to those of common stockholders, including a preference in receiving dividends. Preferred stock is often used by companies to raise capital without diluting the voting rights of common stockholders.
Preferred stock is often issued in one of two forms: convertible preferred stock or non-convertible preferred stock. Convertible preferred stock can be converted into common stock at the option of the holder, usually at any time. Non-convertible preferred stock does not allow the holder to convert their shares into common stock.
Preferred stockholders typically do not have voting rights, but may have a number of rights senior to those of common stockholders. These rights may include:
• Liquidation preference: Preferred stockholders are entitled to receive a specified amount per share in the event of liquidation or dissolution of the company. This amount is usually equal to the par value of the preferred stock.
• Dividend preference: Preferred stockholders are entitled to receive dividends at a specified rate when, as, and if declared by the board of directors. These dividends are usually cumulative, meaning that if dividends are not declared in a particular year, they will accrue and be payable in a future year.
• Redemption rights: Preferred stockholders may have the right to require the company to redeem their shares at a specified price. This price is usually equal to the par value of the preferred stock plus a premium.
• Conversion rights: Preferred stockholders may have the right to convert their shares into common stock at a specified conversion rate. This rate is usually based on market conditions and is intended to approximate fair value.
Preferred stock can be further divided into different classes, each of which may have its own rights, preferences, and privileges. The rights of the holders of preferred stock are determined by the charter documents of the company and are therefore subject to significant variation. The terms of preferred stock are usually established by the board of directors prior to issuance.
Preferred stock is often used by companies to raise capital without diluting the voting rights of common stockholders. For example, a company may issue preferred stock to raise capital for a specific project or to fund a merger or acquisition. The preferred stock may be convertible into common stock at a later date, usually at the option of the holder. This allows the company to raise capital without immediately diluting the ownership of its common stockholders.
Preferred stock may also be used to discourage hostile takeovers. For example, a company may issue preferred stock with rights that are exercisable only in the event of a hostile takeover. These rights may include the ability to purchase additional shares of common stock at a discount to the prevailing market price. This would make it more difficult for a hostile party to acquire a majority of the company's voting stock.
Preferred stock may also be used to fund employee stock ownership plans (ESOPs). ESOPs are qualified retirement plans whereby companies receive a deduction for contributions made to the plan, and plan assets are invested primarily in company stock. Preferred stock may be issued by the company to the ESOP in exchange for employer contributions and then allocated to plan participants based on their years of service and compensation. The preferred stock is convertible into common stock at a specified rate, usually based on market conditions.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Pricing - Market Forces
Stock prices are determined by a variety of factors, including supply and demand, economic conditions, and company performance. These factors interact in complex ways to shape the market forces that influence stock prices. Understanding these market forces is crucial for making informed investment decisions.
**Supply and Demand**
The price of a stock is primarily determined by the forces of supply and demand. When demand for a stock exceeds its supply, the price tends to rise. Conversely, when supply exceeds demand, the price tends to fall. The supply of a stock is largely fixed in the short term, as there are only a certain number of shares outstanding. However, demand can fluctuate rapidly due to changes in investor sentiment, economic conditions, or company-specific events.
For example, consider a company that announces a breakthrough product. This news may cause a surge in demand for the company's stock, driving up its price. Conversely, if the company announces a significant delay in product development, demand may decrease, causing the stock price to fall.
**Economic Conditions**
Economic conditions also play a significant role in shaping stock prices. In a strong economy, companies tend to perform well, and investors are more likely to buy stocks. In a weak economy, companies may struggle, and investors may sell their shares. Interest rates, inflation, and changes in laws (including regulations) can also impact economic conditions and, subsequently, stock prices.
For instance, during periods of high inflation, companies may struggle to maintain profitability. As a result, stock prices may decline. In periods of low interest rates, investors may seek higher returns in the stock market, causing prices to rise.
**Company Performance**
A company's stock price is also influenced by its performance and prospects. Investors closely follow earnings reports, product development, and other business activities. Positive earnings reports or product launches can cause stock prices to rise, while negative earnings or product failures can cause them to fall.
For example, if a company reports higher-than-expected earnings, its stock price may increase. Conversely, if a company reports lower-than-expected earnings, its stock price may decrease.
**Market Forces in Action**
The interplay between supply and demand, economic conditions, and company performance creates complex market forces that influence stock prices. These forces can result in rapid price changes, making it essential for investors to stay informed and adapt to changing market conditions.
Case Study: The Impact of Economic Conditions on Stock Prices
During the 2008 global financial crisis, stock prices plummeted as economic conditions deteriorated. Many companies reported significant losses, and investor confidence was low. As a result, stock prices declined sharply, with some companies experiencing declines of over 70%. However, as economic conditions improved in the following years, stock prices began to rise, and many companies recovered their losses.
* Monitor economic indicators, such as GDP growth, inflation, and interest rates, to understand their impact on stock prices.
* Keep up-to-date with company-specific news and events that may impact stock prices.
* Diversify your portfolio to minimize the impact of any one stock or sector on your overall returns.
Key Takeaways:
* Stock prices are determined by the interplay between supply and demand, economic conditions, and company performance.
* Economic conditions, such as interest rates and inflation, can significantly impact stock prices.
* Company performance, including earnings reports and product development, also influences stock prices.

Stock Pricing and Valuation Techniques
Stock pricing and valuation are critical components of financial analysis. Accurate valuation of stocks is essential for making informed investment decisions. This chapter discusses three primary techniques for stock valuation: discounted cash flow, relative valuation, and asset-based approaches.
Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Approach
The discounted cash flow approach estimates a stock's intrinsic value by calculating the present value of expected future cash flows. This method considers the time value of money and the risk associated with expected cash flows. The DCF model requires making assumptions about future cash flows, discount rates, and terminal values. The model can be applied in two ways:
1. Dividend Discount Model (DDM): This model values a stock based on the present value of expected dividends. It is suitable for companies with a history of paying dividends and a relatively stable dividend payout policy.
2. Free Cash Flow to Equity (FCFE) Model: This model values a stock based on the present value of expected free cash flows to equity. It is suitable for companies with a history of generating free cash flows and a relatively stable capital structure.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Trading - Trading Strategies
Stock trading involves buying and selling shares of publicly traded companies on stock exchanges. It requires a deep understanding of the financial markets, trading strategies, and the ability to manage risk. This chapter covers the basics of stock trading, including day trading, swing trading, and long-term investing.
**Day Trading**
Day trading involves buying and selling financial instruments within a single trading day. This strategy aims to profit from the fluctuations in the market prices of securities during a trading session. Day traders close out their positions before the market closes to avoid overnight risks.
- **Liquidity**: The ability to buy or sell an asset quickly and at a fair price. Liquidity is crucial for day traders as it allows them to enter and exit trades rapidly.
- **Volatility**: The extent to which the price of a security fluctuates. Day traders seek high volatility to maximize their profits.
- **Technical Analysis**: The study of charts and patterns to predict future price movements. Day traders rely heavily on technical analysis to make trading decisions.
- **Risk Management**: The process of identifying, assessing, and mitigating financial risks. Day traders must manage their risk carefully to avoid significant losses.
**Day Trading Strategies:**
- **Scalping**: This involves making a large number of small trades in a short period, taking advantage of the bid-ask spread.
- **Range Trading**: Identifying a range in which a stock is trading and buying or selling based on the expectation that the price will bounce off the upper or lower end of the range.
- **Breakout Trading**: Buying a stock when it breaks out of a established trading range or other resistance level, or selling a stock when it breaks down below a support level.
- **Momentum Trading**: Buying a stock that is moving in an upward trend or selling a stock that is moving in a downward trend.

The Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) is the primary regulatory body responsible for regulating stock exchanges and broker-dealers in the United States. The SEC's primary objective is to protect investors and maintain fair and orderly markets. It was established in 1934 under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, which was enacted in response to the stock market crash of 1929. The SEC oversees the financial services industry, including banks, investment banks, insurance companies, mutual funds, and other financial intermediaries. It also regulates the activities of broker-dealers, investment advisors, and other market participants.
The SEC's regulatory framework for stock markets encompasses a wide range of activities, including:
1. **Registration of Securities**: The SEC requires companies to register their securities before they can be traded on public exchanges. This registration process involves filing a registration statement with the SEC, which includes detailed information about the company, its business, and the securities being offered. The registration statement must be declared effective by the SEC before the securities can be sold.
2. **Broker-Dealer Regulation**: The SEC regulates broker-dealers, who act as intermediaries between investors and the stock market. Broker-dealers must register with the SEC and comply with regulatory requirements, including capital requirements, record-keeping, and reporting obligations. The SEC also regulates broker-dealers' conduct in the sale of securities to investors.
3. **Insider Trading Regulations**: The SEC enforces laws prohibiting insider trading, which involves trading securities based on material non-public information. The SEC has the authority to impose civil and criminal penalties for insider trading violations.
4. **Financial Reporting and Disclosure**: The SEC requires companies to file periodic reports with the SEC, including annual reports (Form 10-K), quarterly reports (Form 10-Q), and current reports (Form 8-K). These reports provide information about a company's financial condition, results of operations, and other matters. The SEC also requires companies to disclose certain information to investors, such as proxy statements and other information regarding corporate governance.
5. **Regulation of Stock Exchanges**: The SEC regulates the operations of stock exchanges, including the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and NASDAQ. The SEC oversees the exchanges' regulatory activities, including surveillance of trading activities and surveillance of listed companies.
6. **Regulation of Market Participants**: The SEC regulates the activities of market participants, including broker-dealers, investment banks, and other market makers. The SEC oversees their conduct in the sale of securities to investors and their compliance with regulatory requirements.
7. **Enforcement**: The SEC has the authority to investigate and bring enforcement actions against companies and individuals for violations of federal securities laws. The SEC can impose civil penalties, including fines and the disgorgement of profits, and can also refer cases to the Department of Justice for criminal prosecution.
8. **Regulation of Investment Banking**: The SEC regulates investment banks, which act as intermediaries between companies and investors in the capital-raising process. The SEC oversees investment banks' conduct in the sale of securities to investors.
9. **Regulation of Mutual Funds**: The SEC regulates mutual funds, which are investment vehicles that pool money from many investors to invest in a variety of assets. The SEC oversees mutual funds' compliance with regulatory requirements, including investment restrictions, disclosure requirements, and reporting obligations.
10. **Regulation of Corporate Governance**: The SEC regulates corporate governance practices, including the composition of corporate boards of directors, executive compensation, and shareholder rights. The SEC has the authority to impose rules and regulations regarding corporate governance practices.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Market Analysis - Technical Analysis
Technical analysis is a method of evaluating securities by analyzing statistics generated by market activity, such as past prices and volume. It is based on the idea that market trends and patterns repeat themselves over time and that by identifying these patterns, investors can anticipate future price movements. Technical analysis is often used in conjunction with fundamental analysis, which focuses on a company's financial statements and business prospects.
- **Trend Analysis**: Identifying and analyzing price trends to anticipate future price movements. Trends can be upward, downward, or sideways.
- **Support and Resistance**: Identifying levels of support (where prices tend to bounce back up) and resistance (where prices tend to fall back down) to anticipate price movements.
- **Chart Patterns**: Identifying specific patterns in price charts to anticipate future price movements. Common patterns include Head and Shoulders, Inverse Head and Shoulders, Triangles, and Wedges.
- **Indicators**: Using mathematical formulas to generate buy and sell signals based on price and volume data. Common indicators include Moving Averages, Relative Strength Index (RSI), and Bollinger Bands.
- **Candlestick Analysis**: Analyzing the shape and color of candlesticks to identify patterns and anticipate future price movements.
**Trend Analysis**
A trend is a sustained direction in the price of a security over a period of time. Identifying trends is crucial in technical analysis as it helps investors anticipate future price movements. There are three main types of trends:
- **Uptrend**: A series of higher highs and higher lows.
- **Downtrend**: A series of lower highs and lower lows.
- **Sideways Trend**: A series of price movements within a narrow range, with no clear direction.
**Identifying Trends**
- **Higher Highs and Higher Lows**: An uptrend is confirmed when a security's price reaches a higher high than the previous high and a higher low than the previous low.
- **Lower Highs and Lower Lows**: A downtrend is confirmed when a security's price reaches a lower high than the previous high and a lower low than the previous low.
- **Trend Lines**: Drawing a line connecting a series of higher lows in an uptrend or lower highs in a downtrend to visualize the trend.
- **Trend Strength**: Measuring the steepness of a trend line to determine the strength of the trend.
**Support and Resistance**
Support and resistance levels are areas on a chart where the price of a security tends to bounce back up or fall back down. These levels can be used to anticipate price movements.
- **Support**: A level where the price of a security tends to bounce back up after falling.
- **Resistance**: A level where the price of a security tends to fall back down after rising.
- **Identifying Support and Resistance**: Looking for areas where the price of a security has previously bounced back up or fallen back down.
- **Breakouts**: When the price of a security breaks through a resistance level, it may continue to rise. When the price of a security breaks through a support level, it may continue to fall.
**Chart Patterns**
Chart patterns are specific formations that appear on a price chart and can be used to anticipate future price movements.
- **Head and Shoulders**: A reversal pattern indicating a potential change in trend. It consists of three peaks, with the middle peak being the highest.
- **Inverse Head and Shoulders**: A reversal pattern indicating a potential change in trend. It consists of three troughs, with the middle trough being the lowest.
- **Triangles**: A continuation pattern indicating that the current trend is likely to continue. It consists of two converging trend lines.
- **Wedges**: A reversal pattern indicating a potential change in trend. It consists of two converging trend lines that slope in the same direction.
**Indicators**
Indicators are mathematical formulas that generate buy and sell signals based on price and volume data.
- **Moving Averages**: Averages of a security's price over a specified period of time. Moving averages can be used to identify trends and generate buy and sell signals.
- **Relative Strength Index (RSI)**: Measures the magnitude of recent price changes to determine overbought or oversold conditions.
- **Bollinger Bands**: A volatility indicator that consists of a moving average and two standard deviations plotted above and below it. It can be used to identify overbought or oversold conditions.
**Candlestick Analysis**
- **Candlestick Patterns**: Specific formations of candlesticks that can be used to anticipate future price movements. Common patterns include Hammer, Shooting Star, and Engulfing.
- **Candlestick Colors**: Green candles indicate an increase in price, while red candles indicate a decrease in price.
- **Use Multiple Time Frames**: Analyzing charts in multiple time frames can provide a more comprehensive view of market trends and patterns.
- **Combine Technical and Fundamental Analysis**: Combining technical analysis with fundamental analysis can provide a more complete view of a security's prospects.
- **Avoid Over-Reliance on Technical Analysis**: Technical analysis should be used in conjunction with other forms of analysis and should not be relied upon solely for investment decisions.
- **Be Disciplined**: Sticking to a disciplined approach to technical analysis can help avoid impulsive decisions based on emotions.
- **Continuously Learn**: Continuously learning and refining technical analysis skills is essential to improve investment outcomes.

1.1. Overview of Financial Markets
Financial markets are platforms where individuals, corporations, and governments trade financial assets. These markets facilitate the flow of capital between savers and borrowers. Financial markets can be broadly classified into two categories: money markets and capital markets. Money markets deal with short-term debt securities, while capital markets involve long-term debt and equity securities. The stock market is a critical component of the capital market, where companies raise capital by issuing shares of stock to the public or to institutional investors.
1.2. Stock Markets
Stock markets, also known as equity markets, are platforms where companies raise capital by issuing shares of stock to the public or to institutional investors. The stock market provides a mechanism for investors to buy and sell securities. The stock market is often seen as a barometer of the economy, reflecting investor sentiment about the prospects of companies and the economy as a whole. Stock markets can be further divided into two categories: primary markets and secondary markets. In primary markets, companies issue new securities to raise capital. In secondary markets, existing securities are traded among investors.
1.3. Stock Market Analysis
Stock market analysis is the process of evaluating a company's financial statements and business prospects to determine the value of its stock. It involves analyzing a company's financial statements, management team, industry, and competitive position, as well as economic and market trends. The goal of stock market analysis is to determine whether a company's stock is overvalued or undervalued compared to its intrinsic value. There are two primary approaches to stock market analysis: fundamental analysis and technical analysis.
1.4. Fundamental Analysis
Fundamental analysis involves evaluating a company's financial statements and business prospects to determine the value of its stock. This approach considers various factors, including the company's financial position, management team, industry, competitive position, and economic and market trends. Fundamental analysts believe that a company's stock price will eventually reflect its intrinsic value. They seek to identify undervalued companies with strong business prospects by analyzing key financial metrics and ratios.
1.5. Key Financial Statements
The following financial statements are essential for fundamental analysis:
1. Balance Sheet
The balance sheet provides a snapshot of a company's financial position at a given point in time. It lists the company's assets, liabilities, and equity. Assets include cash, inventory, property, plant, and equipment, and investments. Liabilities include accounts payable, loans, and other debt. Equity represents the company's ownership interest.
2. Income Statement
The income statement summarizes a company's revenues and expenses over a given period. It provides information about a company's ability to generate earnings compared to its expenses and taxes. The income statement includes revenues, cost of sales, selling, general, and administrative expenses, interest expense, taxes, and net income.
3. Cash Flow Statement
The cash flow statement shows the changes in a company's cash position over a given period. It classifies cash receipts and payments according to operating, investing, and financing activities. Operating activities include cash flows from net income, changes in working capital, and other items. Investing activities include purchases or sales of investments, property, plant, and equipment, and other long-term assets. Financing activities include changes in debt and equity.
4. Statement of Stockholders' Equity
The statement of stockholders' equity summarizes changes in a company's equity over a given period. It includes information about dividends declared, shares issued or repurchased, and changes in retained earnings.
1.6. Financial Ratios
Financial ratios are used to evaluate a company's performance and prospects. The following are some common financial ratios used in fundamental analysis:
1. Price-to-Earnings Ratio (P/E Ratio)
The P/E ratio is calculated by dividing the stock price by earnings per share. It provides a measure of how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of earnings.
2. Price-to-Book Ratio (P/B Ratio)
The P/B ratio is calculated by dividing the stock price by book value per share. It provides a measure of how much investors are willing to pay for each dollar of book value.
3. Dividend Yield
The dividend yield is calculated by dividing the annual dividend per share by the stock price. It provides a measure of the return on investment from dividends.
4. Return on Equity (ROE)
ROE is calculated by dividing net income by total stockholders' equity. It provides a measure of a company's profitability from shareholders' perspective.
5. Return on Assets (ROA)
ROA is calculated by dividing net income by total assets. It provides a measure of a company's profitability from an asset utilization perspective.
6. Debt-to-Equity Ratio
The debt-to-equity ratio is calculated by dividing total debt by total equity. It provides a measure of a company's leverage.
7. Current Ratio
The current ratio is calculated by dividing current assets by current liabilities. It provides a measure of a company's liquidity.
8. Interest Coverage Ratio
The interest coverage ratio is calculated by dividing earnings before interest and taxes by interest expense. It provides a measure of a company's ability to meet its interest obligations.
9. Asset Turnover Ratio
The asset turnover ratio is calculated by dividing revenues by total assets. It provides a measure of a company's efficiency in using its assets to generate revenues.
10. Gross Margin Ratio
The gross margin ratio is calculated by dividing gross profit by revenues. It provides a measure of a company's pricing strategy and operating efficiency.

Competitive analysis involves evaluating a company's competitive position within its industry. It includes an assessment of the company's strengths, weaknesses, opportunities, and threats.

Economic analysis involves evaluating the impact of macroeconomic conditions on a company's business prospects. It includes an assessment of economic indicators such as GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, and unemployment rates. Market analysis involves evaluating the impact of market trends and investor sentiment on a company's stock price.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Market History - The Great Depression
The Great Depression, a global economic downturn that lasted from 1929 to the late 1930s, was one of the most significant economic events of the 20th century. It was triggered by the stock market crash of 1929, which led to a sharp decline in asset values and a loss of investor confidence. This period saw widespread unemployment, business failures, and a significant decline in international trade. The Great Depression had a profound impact on the global economy and led to a re-evaluation of economic theories and policies.
The Roaring Twenties
The 1920s, also known as the Roaring Twenties, were a period of economic prosperity in the United States. The stock market experienced a significant boom, with stock prices rising to unprecedented levels. This period saw the widespread use of margin buying, where investors would purchase stocks with loans from brokers, using the purchased stocks as collateral. Many investors, including ordinary citizens, invested heavily in the stock market, hoping to make quick profits. This led to a surge in stock prices, which created a false sense of security and encouraged more speculation.
The Stock Market Crash of 1929
On Black Tuesday, October 29, 1929, stock prices began to fall dramatically, leading to a wave of panic selling. The stock market continued to decline over the next few days, wiping out millions of dollars in investments. The stock market crash marked the beginning of the Great Depression. The crash led to a loss of investor confidence, causing stock prices to fall further and leading to a sharp decline in economic activity.
Causes of the Great Depression
The causes of the Great Depression are still debated among economists and historians. Some of the contributing factors include:
• Overproduction and underconsumption: Many industries had expanded production and built up large inventories in anticipation of future demand. However, consumer demand was insufficient to absorb the available goods, leading to excess inventory and eventual cutbacks in production.
• Credit crisis: The widespread use of credit to finance consumer purchases and margin loans for stock speculation had created a credit crisis. When the stock market began to decline, many borrowers were unable to repay their loans, leading to a sharp decrease in lending and spending.
• Global economic conditions: The global economy was already facing challenges, including a decline in agricultural prices, protectionist policies, and declining international trade.
• Monetary policy: The monetary policies of central banks, particularly the Federal Reserve in the United States, have been criticized for exacerbating the economic downturn. The Federal Reserve raised interest rates in 1928 and 1929 to combat perceived inflationary pressures, which reduced borrowing and spending.
• Banking system failures: Many banks had invested heavily in the stock market and had loaned money to speculators. When stock prices fell, banks found themselves with large amounts of worthless stocks or loans to insolvent borrowers. As a result, many banks failed, which further reduced lending and spending.
The Great Depression
The Great Depression lasted for over a decade and had a profound impact on the global economy. Some of the key features of the Great Depression include:
• Unemployment: Unemployment rates soared, reaching as high as 25% in the United States. In some countries, unemployment rates exceeded 33%.
• Business failures: Many businesses, including banks, failed as a result of reduced economic activity.
• International trade: International trade declined significantly as countries imposed protectionist policies, such as higher tariffs, to protect their domestic industries.
• Global economic output: Global economic output declined by approximately 15%.
• Social impact: The Great Depression had a significant social impact, including increased levels of poverty, homelessness, and crime.
The Great Depression ended with the onset of World War II. Governments increased spending and implemented expansionary monetary policies to finance their war efforts. This led to increased economic activity and eventually pulled the global economy out of the Great Depression.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Market Crashes - The Great Crash of 1929
The Great Crash of 1929, also known as Black Tuesday, was a global stock market crash that occurred on October 29, 1929. It was the most devastating stock market crash in history, leading to the Great Depression. The crash began on Black Thursday, October 24, 1929, with stock prices falling sharply. The market continued to decline over the next few days, culminating in a massive sell-off on Black Tuesday. The Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) fell by 12.8% on that day, equivalent to approximately $14 billion in today's dollars.
Causes of the Crash
The causes of the Great Crash of 1929 were multifaceted:
1. **Overproduction and Underconsumption**: In the 1920s, there was a surge in industrial production, leading to a surplus of goods. However, many Americans were unable to afford these goods, resulting in a mismatch between supply and demand.
2. **Credit Crisis**: Many investors purchased stocks on margin, meaning they borrowed money to buy stocks. When stock prices began to fall, investors were unable to meet their margin calls, leading to a credit crisis.
3. **Speculation**: Speculation and market manipulation were prevalent in the 1920s. Investors would buy stocks on the expectation of selling them at higher prices, without regard for the underlying value of the companies.
4. **Lack of Regulation**: The stock market was largely unregulated, allowing for fraudulent practices and market manipulation.
5. **Global Economic Conditions**: The global economy was already weakening in 1929, with declining agricultural prices and rising unemployment in Europe.
6. **Federal Reserve Policy**: The Federal Reserve, led by Chairman Benjamin Strong, raised interest rates in 1928 and 1929 to combat perceived inflationary pressures. This reduced the availability of credit and increased interest rates, making it more expensive for investors to finance their stock purchases.
8. **Protectionist Policies**: The passage of the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act in 1930, which raised tariffs on imported goods, is also seen by many as a contributing factor to the Great Depression. The act led to retaliatory measures by other countries, resulting in a sharp decline in international trade.
9. **Wealth Disparity**: The wealth generated during the Roaring Twenties was concentrated among the top 1% of the population. This led to a significant wealth disparity, which limited consumer demand.
10. **Lack of Diversification**: Many investors put all their savings into the stock market, leaving them vulnerable to significant losses.
The Aftermath
The Great Crash of 1929 had a profound impact on the global economy:
1. **Global Economic Downturn**: The crash led to a global economic downturn that lasted for over a decade. The global GDP contracted by an estimated 15%.
2. **Unemployment**: Unemployment soared, with the U.S. unemployment rate rising from 3.2% in 1929 to 24.9% in 1933.
3. **Bank Failures**: Over 9,000 banks failed in the United States between 1929 and 1933. In 1933, President Franklin D. Roosevelt declared a national bank holiday, closing all banks for a week to prevent further bank failures.
4. **Global Trade**: International trade declined by 65% between 1929 and 1934.
5. **Great Depression**: The crash marked the beginning of the Great Depression, which lasted for over a decade. The global economy did not recover until the onset of World War II.
6. **Monetary Policy**: The crash led to significant changes in monetary policy. The Federal Reserve implemented expansionary monetary policies, including lowering interest rates and increasing the money supply.
7. **Regulatory Reforms**: The crash led to significant regulatory reforms, including the creation of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) in 1934. The SEC was established to regulate the stock market and prevent such a disaster from occurring again.
8. **Fiscal Policy**: Governments implemented expansionary fiscal policies, including increased government spending and lower taxes.
9. **Global Cooperation**: The crash led to increased global cooperation, with the establishment of the Bretton Woods system in 1944. The Bretton Woods system established a new international monetary order, with fixed exchange rates and the creation of the International Monetary Fund (IMF).
10. **Long-term Impact**: The crash led to a shift in economic theory, with the development of Keynesian economics. It also led to the creation of the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) in 1933, which insures bank deposits up to $250,000.

Financial Markets and Instruments - Stock Markets - Stock Market Crashes - The Black Monday Crash of 1987
The Black Monday Crash of 1987 was a global stock market crash that occurred on October 19, 1987. It was one of the most significant financial events of the 20th century, with stock markets around the world experiencing sharp declines in a matter of hours. The crash began in Hong Kong and spread to Europe and the United States, with the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) plummeting 22.6% in a single trading day. This event was unprecedented in its scale and speed, causing widespread panic among investors and financial institutions.
Several factors contributed to the Black Monday Crash. One of the primary causes was the use of computer-driven trading strategies, which were relatively new at the time. These strategies, known as program trading, used algorithms to rapidly execute trades based on predefined market conditions. On Black Monday, these algorithms exacerbated market volatility by automatically selling stocks as prices fell, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of selling. Another contributing factor was the lack of liquidity in the market, which made it difficult for investors to sell their shares. **Background**
In the early 1600s, tulips had become a popular luxury item in the Netherlands, particularly among the wealthy. As tulip breeding produced more varieties, rare bulbs became highly sought after, and prices began to rise. By 1634, tulips were traded on the exchanges of numerous Dutch cities, with people of all social classes participating. The rarest varieties, such as the Semper Augustus, were traded for exorbitant prices, with some bulbs selling for as much as 10 times the annual income of a skilled craftsman.
**The Bubble Forms**
The tulip trade evolved from a simple commodity market to a futures market. Contracts to buy bulbs at the end of the growing season were sold without the actual flowers in hand. The contracts, known as "windhandel," were traded on the exchanges of Amsterdam, Haarlem, Leiden, Alkmaar, Rotterdam, and other Dutch cities. People would purchase contracts to buy or sell tulips at the end of the season, forcing up prices. This futures trading meant that there was no longer a limit on the number of tulips involved in the price-setting process, since no physical tulips were required to trade. As a result, prices skyrocketed.
**Speculation and Excess**
As prices rose, people bought up contracts to sell at even higher prices, without intending to sell physical tulips. This was the beginning of the speculative bubble. Contracts were used as collateral for bank loans, and soon, the tulip market had created a credit bubble. The rarest bulbs were selling for small fortunes. At the peak of the mania in 1637, some varieties of tulips were selling for more than 10 times the annual income of a skilled craftsman, causing a sense of unease among the population.
In February 1637, a shipment of tulip bulbs from the Netherlands arrived in the port of Haarlem. Expecting lower prices because of the increased supply, many contracts were defaulted on. As a result, the prices of tulips plummeted. The trade in tulips ground to a halt, leaving many with significant losses. The Dutch government attempted to resolve the situation by allowing courts to declare contracts unenforceable, thus effectively ending the trade. However, the damage had already been done. The economy of the Netherlands was severely impacted, and the tulip trade never recovered.
